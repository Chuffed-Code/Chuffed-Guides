Linux is one kernel and a thousand distributions, but the lineage is short. Almost every distro you'll meet descends from one of a handful of roots. Hover any card, and scroll to the bottom for the whole branching tree.

## Debian family

## Thirty years of distros, branched

One kernel at the top, thirty-two years running down the side, every distro above plotted at the year it first shipped with a line back to its parent. Slackware and Debian split off first in 1993, Red&nbsp;Hat Linux in 1994, Arch and Gentoo shortly after — and then four separate explosions from Debian, Fedora, RHEL, and Arch that cover nearly everything you can install today. The one dashed line is Red&nbsp;Hat Linux (1994–2003), which doesn't exist as a product anymore but fathered both Fedora and RHEL before it stopped.

Most of the rest of the Linux world is a fork of a fork of one of those. If you can place a new distro in this map within thirty seconds — its family, its package manager, its release cadence, its decade — you already know most of what makes it different.
