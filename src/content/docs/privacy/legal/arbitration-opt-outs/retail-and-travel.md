### Airbnb

**✗ No opt-out available** · *Deadline: Check current TOS*

Airbnb has aggressively enforced its arbitration clause, even in a wrongful death case where the victim hadn't booked the rental.

**What it covers:** All disputes related to Airbnb services, including injuries at rentals. The Nevada Supreme Court upheld Airbnb's clause even when the deceased hadn't booked the property.

**How to opt out:**

- Airbnb's current TOS should be checked for any opt-out provisions — they have changed over time.
- As of recent updates, Airbnb has been adding arbitration clauses in more jurisdictions (including Canada).
- Check airbnb.com/terms for the most current terms.

**Contact:** See airbnb.com/terms for current details

:::note
In Airbnb v. Rice (2022), the Nevada Supreme Court ruled in Airbnb's favor, enforcing arbitration in a wrongful death case — even though the man killed hadn't rented the property. The clause was in the TOS he agreed to when creating an account.
:::

### Amazon

**✗ No opt-out available** · *Deadline: N/A — Removed*

Amazon REMOVED its forced arbitration clause in July 2021 after facing 75,000+ individual arbitration demands.

**What it covers:** N/A — Amazon's consumer TOS no longer contains mandatory arbitration.

**How to opt out:**

- No action needed — Amazon removed its mandatory arbitration and class action waiver provisions from its consumer Terms of Service in 2021.
- This was a direct response to mass arbitration campaigns where plaintiff attorneys filed tens of thousands of individual claims.
- Note: Some Amazon subsidiaries or services may still have their own arbitration clauses — check individual terms.

**Contact:** N/A

:::note
Amazon's removal of arbitration came after 75,000+ individual demands were filed over Alexa privacy violations. The cost of arbitrating each case individually became more expensive than the class action they were trying to avoid. This is cited as a major success of the mass arbitration strategy.
:::

### Etsy

**📧 Email opt-out** · *Deadline: 30 days (new users only)*

Etsy allows a 30-day email opt-out — but ONLY for new users. Existing users who didn't opt out by September 2024 are now locked in.

**What it covers:** All disputes with Etsy (including Pattern, the mobile apps, and related services) for users in North and South America. Class action waiver included. Includes batch arbitration rules for mass claims (groups of up to 100 claimants).

**How to opt out:**

- Email: arbitrationoptout@etsy.com
- Must be sent within 30 days of first accepting the Terms.
- Include: your name, the email on your account, your username, your shop name (if applicable), and a clear statement that you're opting out of arbitration.
- IMPORTANT: The September 2024 TOS update removed the opt-out window for existing users. If you were already an Etsy user before Sept 2024 and didn't opt out by then, you cannot opt out now.
- If you previously validly opted out, Etsy says it will continue to respect that opt-out even through future TOS updates.

**Contact:** arbitrationoptout@etsy.com

:::note
Etsy quietly stripped the existing-user opt-out window in its Sept 15, 2024 TOS update — a sharp departure from the previous terms that gave all users 30 days to opt out after each material change. For cases under $25,000, arbitration is now documents-only (no hearing). For $25,000+, videoconference only. Opting out of arbitration routes disputes to New York County, NY state or federal court.
:::

### Gelato

**✗ No opt-out available** · *Deadline: N/A*

Gelato is a Norwegian print-on-demand company. Disputes go to ICC international arbitration seated in Gelato's home city (Oslo). EU consumer protections may apply for individuals.

**What it covers:** All disputes arising from the Gelato Terms, including merchant subscription, orders, and platform use. Governed by the law of the country where Gelato is incorporated (Norway).

**How to opt out:**

- Gelato does NOT offer an opt-out of its arbitration clause.
- The arbitration clause does NOT prevent Gelato from suing in the user's local courts to collect payment or enforce IP rights — but users have no such reciprocal right.
- If you use the 'Buy for Yourself' consumer feature, you may be able to bring consumer-rights claims in the courts of your own country under EU/local consumer law.
- For commercial use, your only practical remedy is ICC arbitration in Oslo — prohibitively expensive for most individual disputes.

**Contact:** See gelato.com/legal

:::note
ICC arbitration typically costs tens of thousands of dollars in filing and administrative fees alone, plus travel to Oslo, making this clause effectively claim-proof for anything under five or six figures. This is a structural barrier, not just a legal one. EU-based consumers have stronger local protections than US merchants.
:::

### Printify

**✗ No opt-out available** · *Deadline: N/A*

Printify (Delaware law) requires AAA mediation followed by binding AAA arbitration. Class actions prohibited. No consumer opt-out.

**What it covers:** All disputes between Printify and merchants/users related to the print-on-demand service. Class and collective actions are waived whether in court, arbitration, or otherwise.

**How to opt out:**

- Printify does NOT offer an opt-out of its arbitration clause.
- Pre-arbitration procedure: contact Printify Merchant Support first, then attempt AAA Commercial Mediation, then AAA Commercial Arbitration.
- If a claim is non-arbitrable, it goes to the US District Court for the District of Delaware.
- Some Printify sub-agreements (events, Dropp*d, etc.) use ICDR international arbitration seated in New York — different forum, same no-opt-out result.

**Contact:** See printify.com/terms-of-service

:::note
Printify uses the AAA Commercial (not Consumer) Arbitration Rules, which are more expensive for individual claimants. If you're an individual merchant with a small dispute, the filing fees alone can exceed the claim value. Delaware venue clause adds travel cost if the dispute proceeds to court.
:::

### Redbubble

**✗ No opt-out available** · *Deadline: N/A*

Redbubble's User Agreement requires binding individual arbitration with a class action waiver. No consumer opt-out. Applies globally, though non-US consumers may retain local consumer protection rights.

**What it covers:** All disputes between users and Redbubble, including purchases, artist earnings, account actions, and the relationship itself. Broadly scoped to include past claims. California law governs.

**How to opt out:**

- Redbubble does NOT offer an opt-out of its arbitration clause.
- Non-US consumers may be entitled to mandatory consumer protection provisions of their local law, which can override parts of the clause.
- Small claims court remains available for qualifying individual claims.
- Injunctive relief for IP disputes can be pursued in court.

**Contact:** See redbubble.com/agreement

:::note
Redbubble's arbitration clause is unusually broad — it covers claims from before you ever agreed to the current Terms. The platform is known for aggressive IP takedowns on artist accounts, which can result in account termination and lost earnings. All of those disputes funnel into arbitration, individually. Redbubble also owns TeePublic.
:::

### Shopify (Merchants)

**✉️ Mail opt-out** · *Deadline: 60 days (Shopify Protect & Capital only)*

Shopify's main Merchant TOS contains binding arbitration with a class action waiver and NO general opt-out. Shopify Protect and Shopify Capital have separate 60-day mail opt-outs.

**What it covers:** Main TOS Section 18 — all disputes between merchants and Shopify related to the platform, including payments, processing, account actions, and store suspensions. Shopify Protect and Shopify Capital are governed by separate agreements with their own arbitration clauses.

**How to opt out:**

- Main Shopify TOS: NO opt-out — binding arbitration with class action waiver applies to all merchants.
- Shopify Protect (chargeback protection) AND Shopify Capital (merchant cash advance): 60-day mail opt-out available.
- Address for Protect/Capital opt-out: Shopify, ATTN: General Counsel, 150 Elgin Street, 8th Floor, Ottawa, ON, K2P 1L4, Canada
- Include: full name, address, account number, and the exact statement 'I reject the Arbitration Provision contained in my Shopify [Protect or Capital] Agreement.'
- Must be sent within 60 days of the Effective Date.
- Shopify states that opting out of these specific clauses does NOT affect the rest of the agreement and does NOT affect any other or future arbitration agreements with them.

**Contact:** Shopify, ATTN: General Counsel, 150 Elgin Street, 8th Floor, Ottawa, ON, K2P 1L4, Canada

:::note
The asymmetry here is notable — Shopify gives opt-outs for the financial products (Protect, Capital) because those are more heavily regulated, but not for the core merchant platform where disputes over account suspensions, payment holds, and store closures actually happen. The Ninth Circuit's Briskin v. Shopify jurisdictional ruling limited Shopify's exposure to California privacy suits. Stripe (used by Shopify Payments) has its own separate arbitration clause layered on top.
:::

### TeePublic

**✗ No opt-out available** · *Deadline: N/A*

TeePublic (TP Apparel LLC) requires confidential binding arbitration in San Francisco under ADR Services rules. Class action waiver and 1-year claim deadline. No opt-out.

**What it covers:** All disputes arising from the TeePublic Terms or use of any part of the service (including buyer purchases and artist sales). Class, consolidated, and representative proceedings are prohibited.

**How to opt out:**

- TeePublic's Terms do NOT contain an opt-out provision — accepting the Terms is a condition of using the site.
- TeePublic reserves the right to go to court for its own injunctive relief and intellectual property claims, but members are bound to arbitration.
- Any cause of action must be filed within ONE year of when it arose — a shortened statute of limitations.
- Small claims court may be an option for qualifying disputes if the amount is within that court's jurisdictional limit.
- Disputes with other users (buyers vs. sellers) release TeePublic from all claims.

**Contact:** See teepublic.com/terms for current dispute resolution details

:::note
TeePublic is owned by Redbubble (acquired 2018). The 1-year limitation period is aggressive — most states allow 3-4+ years for contract and tort claims. If you're selling art on the platform, be aware that disputes over account suspensions, earnings, or IP takedowns all funnel into confidential arbitration with no class mechanism.
:::

**What it covers:** All disputes related to Walmart services. A federal judge ruled Walmart could use its delivery app arbitration clause to block a civil rights lawsuit over a false shoplifting accusation.

- Verify directly with Walmart's legal terms before assuming no opt-out exists.

**Contact:** See walmart.com terms for current details

Walmart successfully compelled arbitration in a case where a Black family sued after being falsely accused of shoplifting. The arbitration clause came from a family member's grocery delivery service agreement — not even related to the incident.
:::
