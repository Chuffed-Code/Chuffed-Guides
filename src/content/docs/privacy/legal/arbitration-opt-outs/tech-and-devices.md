### Adobe

**✉️ Mail opt-out** · *Deadline: 30 days*

Adobe has mandatory arbitration in its General Terms of Use with a 30-day opt-out provision.

**What it covers:** All disputes related to Adobe products and services including Creative Cloud, Acrobat, Photoshop, etc.

**How to opt out:**

- Check adobe.com/legal/terms for the current opt-out procedure.
- Typically requires written notice within 30 days of agreeing to terms.
- Mail to the address specified in Adobe's General Terms of Use.
- Include your name, Adobe ID, and a clear opt-out statement.

**Contact:** See adobe.com/legal/terms for current mailing address

:::note
Adobe made headlines in 2024 for controversial TOS changes that appeared to claim broad rights to user content. The company walked back some language after backlash, but the arbitration clause remains.
:::

### Apple (Consumer Products)

**✗ No opt-out available** · *Deadline: N/A — No Clause*

Apple is RARE among major tech companies in NOT including an arbitration clause in its consumer Terms of Service.

**What it covers:** N/A — Apple's consumer TOS and app developer agreements do not contain forced arbitration clauses.

**How to opt out:**

- No action needed — Apple does not include forced arbitration in its consumer TOS.
- Note: Apple DOES have an employee arbitration clause (with opt-out).
- The Apple Card (Goldman Sachs) has its own separate arbitration clause — see that entry.
- Apple Payments (Apple Cash) does contain arbitration — see Apple's legal pages for specifics.

**Contact:** N/A

:::note
Per The American Prospect, Apple is rare among large companies in not including an arbitration clause in consumer TOS or developer contracts. Since 2016, only 2 employees have pursued arbitration against Apple. This is the gold standard other companies should follow.
:::

### Brave Browser

**📧 Email opt-out** · *Deadline: 30 days (Rewards/Ads only)*

Brave's main browser TOS has no arbitration. But Brave Rewards and Brave Ads have mandatory arbitration with a 30-day email opt-out.

**What it covers:** Brave Rewards (BAT tokens) and Brave Ads services only. The basic browser TOS does not contain an arbitration clause.

**How to opt out:**

- For Brave Ads/Advertiser terms: Email advertisers@brave.com within 30 days of accepting terms.
- Include: your full name, address, and a clear statement of intent to opt out of binding arbitration.
- For Brave Rewards (User terms): Check the current terms at basicattentiontoken.org/user-terms-of-service for the specific opt-out process.
- If you only use the Brave browser without Rewards/Ads features, you are likely not bound by arbitration.

**Contact:** advertisers@brave.com (Ads)  |  basicattentiontoken.org (Rewards)

:::note
Brave's approach is layered: the browser itself is relatively arbitration-free, but its crypto/advertising ecosystem (BAT, Rewards) includes mandatory arbitration governed by JAMS rules under Cayman Islands law. If you don't use Brave Rewards, you're in better shape than most browser users.
:::

### Epic Games (Fortnite, Epic Store)

**✉️ Mail opt-out** · *Deadline: 30 days*

Epic Games has mandatory arbitration with a 30-day mail opt-out. Covers Fortnite, Epic Games Store, Rocket League, etc.

**What it covers:** All disputes related to Epic's websites, products, and services. Class action waiver remains even if you opt out of arbitration.

**How to opt out:**

- Mail a written 'Arbitration Opt-Out Notice' to: Epic Games, Inc., Legal Department, ATTN: ARBITRATION OPT-OUT, Box 254, 2474 Walnut Street, Cary, North Carolina 27518
- Include: (a) your full name, Epic Games Account, and email address, (b) a clear statement opting out, and (c) your signature.
- Must be sent within 30 days of signing up or accepting updated terms.
- You can also opt out of future material changes within 30 days of notice.
- NOTE: Even if you opt out of arbitration, the class action waiver still applies.

**Contact:** Epic Games, Inc., Legal Department, ATTN: ARBITRATION OPT-OUT, Box 254, 2474 Walnut Street, Cary, NC 27518

:::note
Epic's arbitration clause was enforced against minors in a 2024 privacy case — 6 of 7 plaintiffs were sent to arbitration. The class action waiver survives opt-out, which limits the value of opting out somewhat, but preserving court access is still worthwhile.
:::

### Google (Chrome, Gmail, consumer services)

**✗ No opt-out available** · *Deadline: N/A — No General Clause*

Google's main consumer TOS (covering Chrome, Gmail, YouTube, Maps, etc.) does NOT contain forced arbitration for general users. Device and Ads terms are separate.

**What it covers:** N/A for general consumer services. Google's general Terms of Service route disputes to California courts, not mandatory arbitration.

**How to opt out:**

- No action needed for Chrome, Gmail, YouTube, Maps, etc. — no arbitration clause in the general consumer TOS.
- HOWEVER: Google Devices (Pixel, Nest, Chromecast) have a separate arbitration agreement with a 30-day opt-out at g.co/devicearbitration/optout.
- Google Ads has its own arbitration clause with a 30-day opt-out at ads.google.com/nav/arbitration.
- Google Fiber also has arbitration with opt-out provisions.
- Always check the specific terms for each Google product you use.

**Contact:** g.co/devicearbitration/optout (devices)  |  ads.google.com/nav/arbitration (ads)

:::note
Google's approach is product-specific. The general consumer TOS is notably clean — disputes go to California courts. But hardware, advertising, and fiber services each have their own arbitration clauses. This is better than Microsoft (blanket arbitration) but less clean than Apple (no consumer arbitration at all).
:::

### Google (Pixel, Nest, Chromecast)

**✓ Has opt-out** · *Deadline: 30 days*

Google provides an online opt-out form for device arbitration. One of the easier opt-out processes.

**What it covers:** Disputes regarding design, performance, features, or functionality of Google devices and related subscription services.

**How to opt out:**

- Visit: g.co/devicearbitration/optout (online form)
- Select the Google product you want to opt out for (Pixel, Nest, Chromecast, etc.).
- Fill out the form with your information.
- Must be completed within 30 days of activating your device for the first time.
- You can also return the device within 30 days instead.

**Contact:** g.co/devicearbitration/optout

:::note
Google's opt-out is notable for being an online form — much simpler than competitors requiring physical mail. Google will reimburse arbitration filing fees for claims under $10,000. Hearings default to Santa Clara County or your county.
:::

### Microsoft (Windows, Xbox, Office, etc.)

**✗ No opt-out available** · *Deadline: N/A*

Microsoft's Services Agreement contains binding arbitration (Section 15) for all US customers. No clear consumer opt-out provision found.

**What it covers:** All disputes related to Microsoft consumer products: Windows, Xbox, Office 365, Outlook, OneDrive, Bing, Edge, Teams, Skype, and all services listed at microsoft.com/servicesagreement.

**How to opt out:**

- Microsoft does NOT appear to offer a consumer opt-out for its arbitration clause.
- You and Microsoft agree to try to resolve disputes informally for 60 days first.
- If unresolved, disputes go to binding individual arbitration before the AAA.
- Microsoft reimburses filing fees and pays AAA costs for disputes under $75,000.
- Small claims court remains an option.
- To start the dispute process: mail a Notice of Dispute to Microsoft Corporation, ATTN: CELA ARBITRATION, One Microsoft Way, Redmond, WA 98052-6399, or submit an Electronic Notice of Dispute form online.

**Contact:** Microsoft Corporation, ATTN: CELA ARBITRATION, One Microsoft Way, Redmond, WA 98052-6399  |  microsoft.com/en-us/legal/arbitration

:::note
Microsoft's arbitration applies to an enormous range of products. The updated September 2025 terms incorporate AAA Mass Arbitration Supplementary Rules. Microsoft provides a dedicated arbitration information page at microsoft.com/en-us/legal/arbitration with forms and product-specific agreements.
:::

### Proton (Mail, VPN, Drive, Pass)

**✉️ Mail opt-out** · *Deadline: 30 days*

Proton added mandatory arbitration in June 2023 — surprising for a privacy-focused company. 30-day opt-out by written notice.

**What it covers:** All disputes related to your Proton account, services, advertising, or communications. Class action waiver included. Applies to US users; non-US users can still bring claims in Swiss court.

**How to opt out:**

- Send written notice of your decision to opt out to the address specified in Proton's current Terms of Service at proton.me/legal/terms.
- Must be sent within 30 calendar days of first agreeing to the terms.
- You can opt out of both arbitration AND the class action waiver, or just arbitration alone.
- You may NOT opt out of only the class action waiver without opting out of arbitration.
- You also have 30 days to opt out after any material changes to the arbitration terms.
- Non-US users: The arbitration clause primarily affects US users. Proton is based in Switzerland and non-US users retain their right to bring claims in Swiss court.

**Contact:** See proton.me/legal/terms for current opt-out mailing address

:::note
Proton's addition of arbitration in 2023 was controversial for a company that markets itself on privacy and user rights. Proton clarified that Swiss law (their legal jurisdiction) doesn't allow class actions anyway, and that US users retain the right to bring claims in Swiss court — the arbitration clause adds an additional US-based option, not a restriction. Many users disagreed with this framing.
:::

### Roku

**✉️ Mail opt-out** · *Deadline: 30 days*

Roku made headlines in 2024 for disabling TVs until users agreed to updated arbitration terms. Mail-only opt-out.

**What it covers:** All disputes with Roku related to products and services. Class action waiver included.

**How to opt out:**

- First, click 'Agree' on the Roku device prompt (required to use the device).
- Then send a physical letter with: your name, address, phone number, email on file with Roku, product/service name, and copies of receipts.
- Mail to the address specified in Roku's Dispute Resolution Terms on your device.
- Must be within 30 days of agreeing to terms.

**Contact:** See current Dispute Resolution Terms on your Roku device for mailing address

:::note
Roku drew widespread criticism in 2024 for effectively bricking users' devices until they agreed to new terms with arbitration clauses. You must agree first, then opt out — you cannot simply reject the terms without losing device functionality.

### Samsung

### Steam / Valve

**✗ No opt-out available** · *Deadline: N/A — Removed*

Valve REMOVED forced arbitration from Steam in September 2024 after facing 75,000+ mass arbitration claims. All disputes now go to court.

**What it covers:** N/A — Steam's Subscriber Agreement no longer contains mandatory arbitration. Disputes go to state or federal courts in King County, Washington.

**How to opt out:**

- No action needed — Valve has eliminated the arbitration requirement.
- They also removed the class action waiver and fee-shifting provisions.
- All disputes now go to courts in King County, Washington.
- This is one of the biggest consumer wins in arbitration history.

**Contact:** N/A

:::note
Valve removed arbitration after law firms filed 75,000+ individual arbitration demands that threatened $225M+ in fees. Like Amazon before it, mass arbitration made forced arbitration more expensive than the class actions companies were trying to avoid. A landmark victory for consumer rights.
:::

### Tuta (Tutanota)

**✗ No opt-out available** · *Deadline: N/A — No Clause*

Tuta is a German email provider governed by German law and the GDPR. No forced arbitration clause in consumer terms — disputes are handled under German jurisdiction with mandatory consumer protection.

**What it covers:** N/A — Tuta's consumer relationship is governed by German law. Consumers in the EU can additionally invoke the consumer protection law of their country of residence.

**How to opt out:**

- No action needed — Tuta does not include forced arbitration in its consumer terms.
- German companies cannot impose gag orders or FAA-style arbitration on EU consumers due to GDPR and EU consumer protection mandates.
- Tuta is subject to German judicial process — only a German court order can compel disclosure of user data, and only for serious criminal investigations (§ 100a StPO).
- The company publishes a semi-annual Transparency Report and a Warrant Canary.

**Contact:** Tutao GmbH, Deisterstr. 17a, 30449 Hannover, Germany  |  hello@tuta.de

:::note
Tuta's jurisdictional position is one of the strongest among privacy-focused email providers. Unlike US-based competitors, there is no FAA preemption, no arbitration clause, and no way for Tuta to be compelled to implement a backdoor. German General Terms law (§§ 305 BGB) is strict — even B2B contracts face stronger consumer-protection-style review than US analogs. The one caveat: Tuta cannot protect against targeted monitoring orders issued by German courts for serious criminal cases (murder, CSAM, terrorism).
:::
