## What data brokers know about you

Data brokers are companies that collect, aggregate, and sell your personal information scraped from public records, social media, purchase histories, and more. They make money by selling your life to anyone willing to pay.

- **👤 Your full profile.** Name, age, date of birth, current and past addresses, phone numbers, email addresses, relatives and associates — all linked together in one searchable profile.
- **💰 Financial signals.** Estimated income, property ownership records, mortgage details, and net worth estimates. Some sites even show your political donations and voter registration.
- **🔓 Used against you.** Stalkers, scammers, identity thieves, aggressive marketers, and social engineers all use data broker sites as their starting point. Your profile is their playbook.
- **♻️ It comes back.** Even after you remove yourself, data brokers re-scrape public records and re-list you within weeks or months. Removal is not a one-time task — it requires a long-term strategy.

## Year 2+: Free Scans + CCPA Letters

Your data is mostly cleaned up. Now you just need to catch the re-listings and kill them with a legal hammer. The California Consumer Privacy Act gives you that hammer — even if you don't live in California.

### Step 1 — Every Quarter: Check Your Exposure Report

Optery Free Basic sends you an Exposure Report approximately every 3 months (as long as you've logged in recently). Open it. It will show any sites where your data has reappeared. This is your hit list.

### Step 2 — For Each Re-Listing: Send a CCPA Demand

For every site that shows your data, send the following email to their contact or privacy address. This invokes the California Consumer Privacy Act, which carries penalties of $2,500–$7,500 per violation. Most companies just remove you rather than risk it.

```
Pursuant to the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), I demand that my personal information be removed from your website. Furthermore, per Part 4 of Division 3 of the California Civil Code (AB-375), I demand a waiver of any payment for this demand.

Please remove the following from your service:

Full Name: [Your Name]
Physical Address: [As it appears on their site]
Telephone: [If displayed]
Email: [If displayed]

My current California address is:

[Your Name]
GENERAL DELIVERY
Los Angeles, CA 90001-9999

I expect confirmation of removal within 30 days.
```

:::tip[The General Delivery trick]
You don't need to live in California. The book's author uses a USPS General Delivery address in Los Angeles — a free, temporary mailing option the USPS provides to people without permanent addresses. Companies rarely verify your California residency, and it's less effort for them to remove you than to risk a $7,500 CCPA fine. In real-world use, companies typically remove the data without even responding.
:::

### Step 3 — For Stubborn Sites: Escalate

If a site doesn't respond to your CCPA demand within a couple weeks, you have escalation options:

- **Option A:** Send the same demand to their web host and domain registrar. Hosting companies will translate your complaint and you become a nuisance they want to resolve.
- **Option B:** Use Optery's Free Basic self-service tools — they provide direct opt-out links and step-by-step guides for DIY removal from each broker.
- **Option C:** For sites that demand a photo ID or "selfie" to process the removal, upload a random AI-generated face from a site like thispersondoesnotexist.com. Most verification is automated and they rarely look at the actual image.

### Step 4 — Submit Search Engine Re-Index Requests

After a broker removes your data, cached versions may still show in Google/Bing search results. Clean these up manually:

- **Google:** Sign into any Google account → go to `search.google.com/search-console/remove-outdated-content` → enter the exact URL → enter a word from your old listing that's no longer on the page → submit. Results clear within ~24 hours.
- **Bing:** Sign into any Microsoft account → go to `bing.com/webmasters/tools/contentremoval` → enter the URL → select "Remove outdated cache" → submit.

### Step 5 — Repeat Every Quarter

That's it. Check your Optery Free Basic report every 3 months, send CCPA letters to any re-listings, clean up search engine caches. About an hour of work per quarter — 4 hours per year — at zero cost. Over time, the re-listings slow down significantly, especially if your PMB is the only address in public records.

## Why the CCPA letter actually works

The California Consumer Privacy Act gives consumers three core rights against businesses: the right to KNOW what data they have, the right to DELETE it, and the right to DEMAND they stop selling it.

**💰 $2,500–$7,500 per violation.** Companies that fail to comply face fines per violation. For data brokers with millions of records, the risk is enormous. It's always cheaper and easier to just remove your listing.

**🏠 General Delivery = your CA address.** USPS General Delivery provides a temporary mailing address to anyone without a permanent one. The Los Angeles General Delivery address (90001-9999) serves as your California presence for CCPA purposes.

**🔍 No verification in practice.** Companies don't verify your California residency — they just process the request. Many large companies (Google, Facebook, Microsoft) now apply CCPA protections to all customers regardless of location anyway.

**📋 Applies to the right targets.** CCPA covers companies with $25M+ annual revenue, 50K+ consumer records, or 50%+ revenue from data sales. This includes virtually every data broker that matters.

:::caution[Limitations]
Companies can claim exemptions (data needed for transactions, legal obligations, security, free speech, research, or internal use). In practice, data brokers rarely invoke these because it's not worth the fight. Smaller sites below the revenue threshold may simply ignore you — use their direct opt-out links instead.
:::

## Why Optery + PMB = actual privacy

A removal service alone isn't enough. Neither is a ghost address alone. Together, they create a self-reinforcing loop that gets stronger over time.

**Without a PMB:** Optery removes your home address from data brokers, but your home address is still on file with your bank, credit cards, and government records. Those public records are exactly what brokers re-scrape. Your data comes right back — from the same source, to the same brokers.

**With a PMB:** You change your official address to the ghost address FIRST. Now when data brokers re-scrape public records, they find your PMB address — not your home. Optery removes the old listings with your home address, and the new listings that appear only show the PMB. Over time, your real home address fades completely from the internet.

**After Year 1:** When you switch to CCPA maintenance, the re-listings are mostly just your PMB address anyway. You're sending CCPA letters to remove a South Dakota mail facility address — not your actual home. Even if a broker is slow to comply, the exposed address is a dead end shared by thousands of people.

:::

## Things to know before signing up

**⚠️ ~68% removal rate.** A 2024 Consumer Reports study found Optery removed about 68% of profiles after four months. Not perfect — but dramatically better than doing nothing, and the remaining 32% is what custom removals and CCPA letters are for.

**🇺🇸 US only.** Optery focuses on US-based data brokers. International brokers require manual removal or a different service.

**🤖 AI feature — leave it off.** Optery offers an optional AI processing feature using OpenAI. When enabled, your personal info is shared with OpenAI. You're trying to remove your data from the internet, not give it to another company. Keep this off.

**🔄 Expanded Reach — use carefully.** Expanded Reach sends "blind opt-outs" to brokers who may not have your data yet. This could introduce your info to new places. Only enable it if you've confirmed those brokers already have you listed.

**📊 ~30% false matches.** About 30% of records Optery finds may be incorrect matches (wrong person, same name). Review your dashboard and flag false positives so removals focus on your actual profiles.

**👥 Separate plans per person.** Each person needs their own subscription. Optery sometimes offers family bundle discounts — check their pricing page.

## Other cleanup to do during Year 1

While Optery is running, take care of these related tasks to stop new data from leaking out.

- **❄️ Freeze your credit.** Freeze your credit at Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. This prevents unauthorized credit pulls and stops new accounts from being opened in your name. Add a fraud alert for extra protection.
- **📬 Opt out of pre-screened offers.** Go to optoutprescreen.com and opt out permanently. This stops pre-approved credit and insurance offers — which are commonly stolen from mailboxes and used for identity fraud.
- **📊 Request your LexisNexis report.** See what the biggest data aggregator has on you: consumer.risk.lexisnexis.com/request. This shows you the full picture of what's stored beyond just people-search sites.
- **🔍 Clean up search engine caches.** Submit re-index requests to Google and Bing for any pages where your data has been removed. Old cached versions can linger in search results for months if you don't actively clear them.
