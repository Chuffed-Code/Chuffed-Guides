**Legal Infrastructure for Maximum Privacy**

How to remove your name from your home deed, vehicle titles, and public records using a revocable trust with an attorney-as-trustee.

:::caution[This is a universal strategy, not legal advice]
The architecture below — generic trust names, attorney as trustee, separated asset classes — applies in every US state. But the specific statutes, witnessing rules, transfer-tax exemptions, and recording practices vary significantly by jurisdiction. This guide tells you *what to build*; a licensed estate planning attorney in your state tells you *exactly how to build it there*. Do not rely on this guide alone for execution.
:::

## The problem: your assets are broadcasting your home address to the world

Right now, your name and home address are attached to your property deed in county public records. Anyone can search the register of deeds online and find exactly where you live. Your vehicle titles and registrations tie your name to your plates — which get captured by license plate readers and sold to data brokers. Your utility accounts connect your name to your address through credit bureaus. And the more visible you are professionally, the bigger a target you become for spam, social engineering, data harvesting, and eventually people who want to find you for reasons you haven't thought of yet.

A **revocable trust** is the legal entity that breaks all of these connections. When your home is owned by "The 48291 Trust" instead of your name, someone searching for you finds nothing. When your car is registered to a trust at a PMB address, a license plate lookup hits a dead end. You still own and control everything — you're just invisible on paper.

> "I never recommend titling a home in a LIVING trust. Your name will likely be filed at the county level in connection with the home and you lose all privacy. I also never recommend placing your home into the same trust that holds other assets."
>
> — Michael Bazzell, *Extreme Privacy*

## The architecture: what Bazzell actually recommends

Most estate planners will suggest a single "living trust" with your name in the title — "The Johnson Family Living Trust" — and put everything in it. This defeats the entire purpose for privacy. Bazzell's approach is different in four critical ways:

### Rule 1: Never use a "living trust" label

Adding "Living Trust" or "Land Trust" or "Property Trust" to the name triggers specific state laws and reveals the trust's purpose. Instead, Bazzell recommends calling it simply **"The [Generic Name/Number] Trust"** — something like "The 48291 Trust" or "The Meridian Holdings Trust." No family name, no purpose identifier, no personal connection.

### Rule 2: Never put your home and other assets in the same trust

If your home and your financial accounts are in the same trust, anyone who discovers the trust name on the county deed can potentially connect it to your bank accounts. **Separate trusts for separate asset classes.** A typical structure:

:::note[Recommended trust structure]
**Trust A** — For the home. Generic name. Attorney as trustee. Recorded at your county register of deeds with the deed. This is the only trust that touches public records.

**Trust B** — For vehicles. Different generic name. You can serve as trustee here since DMV records are typically less publicly searchable than county deed records. PMB as the address.

**Trust C (optional)** — A living trust for financial accounts (investments, savings). You as trustee. Never touches public records. Primarily for probate avoidance at death.
:::

### Rule 3: The trustee matters more than anything

Whoever is listed as trustee is the name that appears on public records alongside the trust name. If you're the trustee of your home trust, the county's deed will say "The 48291 Trust, [Your Name], Trustee" — and now your name is connected to the property again. With an **attorney as trustee**, the county record shows "The 48291 Trust, [Attorney Name], Trustee." Your name appears nowhere in public records. The attorney-client privilege adds yet another layer — even if someone asks the attorney about the trust, they're legally prohibited from disclosing anything.

### Rule 4: No EIN for the trust

Bazzell explicitly warns against obtaining an Employer Identification Number from the IRS for your trust. An EIN creates annual tax reporting requirements and complicates revoking the trust. Since the trust won't generate income, acquire credit, or hire employees, it's unnecessary. All trust transactions flow through your personal tax return.

:::caution[Critical sequence]
Your PMB must be set up and tested BEFORE you create the trust. The trust documents need an address, and you want that to be your ghost address — not your home address. If you put your home address on the trust documents, it defeats the purpose. **PMB first, trust second.**
:::

## The trustee swap technique (the exact order of operations)

This is the smartest approach from Bazzell — it saves you money while maximizing privacy. Here's how the sequence works:

### Step 1 — Set up your PMB

Americas Mailbox (or equivalent) needs to be live and tested before anything else. Your PMB address goes on every trust document as the trust's mailing address.

### Step 2 — Find and retain an estate planning attorney

You need someone who understands privacy-motivated trusts, not just standard estate planning. During the initial consultation, ask: "Can you serve as trustee on my trust for the purpose of keeping my name off the county deed?" If they look confused or refuse, move on. More on finding the right attorney below.

### Step 3 — Attorney creates the trust with YOU as initial trustee

The trust is created as a private document. You are named as grantor, beneficiary, and initial trustee. This is temporary — you're the trustee only for the purpose of opening a trust checking account at a bank. The trust document is never filed publicly at this stage. It sits in your attorney's office and yours.

### Step 4 — Open a trust checking account

Go to a local credit union with your certification of trust (showing you as trustee) and open a checking account in the trust's name. This account will be used for mortgage payments, property taxes, insurance, and utilities — all flowing through the trust name instead of your personal name. The bank knows who you are (KYC), but the account name is the trust.

### Step 5 — Attorney amends the trust — appoints themselves as trustee

Before anything is recorded at the county, the attorney drafts a trust amendment removing you as trustee and appointing themselves. This is a private, notarized document. No public record is created. You remain the grantor and beneficiary with full control — the attorney is just the signatory.

### Step 6 — Execute the quitclaim deed transfer (your home)

The attorney drafts a quitclaim deed transferring your home from your name to the trust. The deed is signed by you (as grantor/current owner), witnessed as required by your state, and notarized. The certification of trust — now showing the attorney as trustee — is attached. Both documents are recorded at your county Register of Deeds.

**What the county record shows:** "The 48291 Trust, [Attorney Name], Trustee" — your name appears nowhere.

### Step 7 — Notify your mortgage lender (don't ask — notify)

Send your lender a letter (your attorney can draft this) informing them that the property has been transferred into your revocable trust for estate planning purposes. Cite the **Garn-St. Germain Depository Institutions Act of 1982** (12 U.S.C. § 1701j-3), which specifically prohibits lenders from enforcing due-on-sale clauses when the borrower transfers property into a trust where they remain the beneficiary and occupant. You are not asking permission. You are informing them of a legally protected transfer.

### Step 8 — Transfer any paid-off vehicles into Trust B

Visit your DMV with the certification of trust for your vehicle trust (separate from the home trust). Request re-titling from your name to the trust. Provide the PMB as the registration address. Most states allow this for vehicles without liens. Any vehicle still under a loan gets transferred when it's paid off.

### Step 9 — Transfer utilities to the trust name

Call each utility provider and request to change the account name to the trust. Provide the certification of trust as proof. Set up auto-pay from the trust checking account. Most utilities accept this without resistance once they see the trust paperwork — they just want to get paid. Power companies are the exception and may require an EIN, which means creating a Sole Proprietorship DBA with the IRS (free, instant) with a name similar to the trust.

### Step 10 — Update insurance to reference the trust

Your homeowner's insurance should be in the name of the trust with you as secondary insured. Your auto insurance stays in your name (the insurer needs your driving history and credit), but the mailing address changes to the PMB, and the policy references the trust as the vehicle owner. An insurance office in the same county as your PMB will often be familiar with this setup.

## Finding the right estate planning attorney

Not every estate attorney will serve as trustee. Most just draft documents and send you on your way. You need someone willing to play an ongoing role. Bazzell describes his own arrangement:

> "My trustee is an attorney who specializes in estate planning. For a fee, he agreed to scrutinize my documents and act as a trustee on my behalf. He signed my closing documents on my home as the trustee of my trust. We possess a private contract eliminating any liability on his behalf. The attorney-client privilege offers yet another layer of trust between us."

### Questions to ask during the initial consultation

**"Do you create revocable trusts for privacy purposes, not just estate planning?"**
: This separates attorneys who understand your goal from those who only do standard probate-avoidance trusts. If they immediately understand the distinction, good sign.

**"Are you willing to serve as trustee on the trust?"**
: This is the make-or-break question. Many will say no. That's fine — keep looking. Some will say yes with conditions. Listen to the conditions carefully.

**"What are your ongoing fees for serving as trustee?"**
: Get this in writing. Annual retainer vs. per-signature billing. Understand what's included and what triggers extra charges.

**"Can we structure a liability waiver so you're protected as trustee?"**
: Bazzell mentions having a "private contract eliminating any liability on his behalf" for the attorney. This protects the attorney and makes them more willing to serve.

**"What does my county require on the deed — just the trust name, or the trustee name too?"**
: The attorney should know this immediately. If the county requires the trustee name, having the attorney's name there instead of yours is the whole point. If they don't require it, even better.

:::tip[How to approach them]
Use your Proton alias email when contacting attorneys. Do NOT give your real home address — use your PMB once it's live. When scheduling, say you're interested in "a revocable trust for privacy and estate planning, with the attorney potentially serving as trustee." If they push back on the trustee role on the first call, move on. You may need to contact 3–5 firms before finding the right fit.
:::

## State-specific questions to ask your attorney

Because real estate law varies enormously by state, the same trust architecture may have different implementation details where you live. Bring this checklist to your attorney consultation:

**Transfer tax / recording fees**
: Does my state exempt transfers into a trust where I remain the beneficiary from the deed recording fee or transfer tax? Many states do. Ask for the specific statute citation so you can verify.

**Witness and notarization requirements**
: How many witnesses does a quitclaim deed need? Does the notary count as a witness? Must the deed reference the existing mortgage lien? Are there state-specific phrases that must appear?

**Certificate of trust vs. full trust**
: Does my county recognize certificates of trust for recording, or does it require the full trust document? The whole point of privacy is to file the abbreviated certificate — not the full trust that identifies you as grantor and beneficiary.

**Warranty deed vs. quitclaim**
: Is a quitclaim deed sufficient for transfer to a trust, or does my state require a warranty deed (which has additional derivation/chain-of-title requirements)?

**Homestead exemption preservation**
: Will I lose my homestead tax exemption when the deed is in the trust's name? Many states preserve it if the trust contains a homestead rights clause and you continue occupying the property. Confirm the specific clause language.

**Vehicle title transfers**
: Does my state's DMV allow vehicle titles in the name of a trust? Does it require the trustee name on the title, or just the trust name? What's the fee per vehicle?

**Property tax records**
: Will the tax assessor's public records show the trust name, the trustee name, both, or something else? Will the bill mail to the PMB without friction?

If your attorney can't answer these quickly, they aren't the right attorney for this work.

## Garn-St. Germain: the one federal protection you can rely on everywhere

Regardless of what state you're in, **12 U.S.C. § 1701j-3** — the Garn-St. Germain Depository Institutions Act of 1982 — specifically prohibits your mortgage lender from calling your loan due when you transfer the property into an inter vivos (living) trust where you remain the beneficiary and continue occupying the property. This is settled federal law that overrides any "due-on-sale" clause in your mortgage contract. Your lender **cannot** accelerate your mortgage or force you to refinance. This applies in every state.

:::caution[Practical warning]
Some lenders — especially large national servicers — send alarming letters when they discover the deed has changed. They may claim the due-on-sale clause has been triggered. This is almost always an automated response from employees unfamiliar with Garn-St. Germain. Your attorney should draft a proactive notification letter citing the statute. If the lender pushes back, the attorney sends a follow-up citing the specific federal code. In practice, lenders always back down because the law is clear. But you need to be prepared for the scary letter so you don't panic.
:::

## Realistic costs

Figures below are rough ranges typical for estate planning attorneys in small/mid-sized US markets. Major metro areas run higher.

**📜 Trust Creation Package — $2,000–$3,500**
: Trust A (home), Trust B (vehicles), certification of trust for each, pour-over will, powers of attorney. Attorney drafts and reviews all documents.

**🏠 Deed Transfer — $300–$600**
: Attorney drafts quitclaim deed, affidavit of value (if required in your state), Garn-St. Germain notification letter. County recording fee typically ~$25. State transfer tax varies — many states exempt trust transfers where the grantor is the beneficiary, but confirm with your attorney.

**⚖️ Attorney-as-Trustee Setup — $500–$1,500**
: Liability waiver contract, trustee acceptance, ongoing availability agreement. One-time fee on top of trust creation for the attorney to agree to serve.

**🚗 Vehicle Transfers (per vehicle) — $15–$50**
: DMV title transfer fee per vehicle. Attorney provides certification of trust copies. Vehicles under loan transfer once paid off.

**📅 Annual Trustee Fee — $200–$500/yr**
: Attorney retainer to remain available as trustee. Some charge a flat annual fee, others bill per-signature at $200–$350/hr. In a quiet year, minimal activity.

**📋 Per-Signature Events — $200–$500/event**
: Refinance, insurance claim, selling a vehicle, any document that requires the trustee's signature. Billed at the attorney's hourly rate. Rare in most years.

:::note[Realistic total budget]
**Year 1 (setup):** $3,000–$5,500 all-in, including trust creation, deed transfer, vehicle transfers, and the attorney-as-trustee agreement.

**Year 2+ (maintenance):** $200–$1,000/year depending on how many trustee signatures you need. In a quiet year with no refinancing or vehicle sales, you might pay nothing beyond the annual retainer.
:::

## What your attorney will create

You don't draft these yourself — your attorney does. But you should understand what each one is so you can review them intelligently. Bazzell provides templates in his book, but emphasizes that **"a competent estate attorney should confirm the most appropriate path for you."**

```
1. Declaration of Trust (Trust A — Home)
   Master trust document. Names you as grantor and beneficiary. Initially names
   you as trustee (later amended). Generic trust name, no personal identifiers.
   Governed by your state's law. Includes homestead rights clause. Never filed
   publicly.

2. Declaration of Trust (Trust B — Vehicles)
   Separate trust with a different generic name. You as grantor, beneficiary,
   and trustee (you can stay as trustee on this one since DMV records are less
   publicly exposed than county deed records). PMB as the trust address.

3. Trust Amendment — Appointment of New Trustee
   Signed and notarized document removing you as trustee of Trust A and
   appointing the attorney. Executed before the deed is recorded. Private
   document, never filed publicly.

4. Certification of Trust (for each trust)
   Abbreviated "proof of existence" document you show to institutions.
   Contains: trust name, date created, trustee name and powers, confirmation
   it's revocable and currently valid. Does NOT contain: your name as grantor,
   beneficiaries, SSN, or asset details. Recorded at the county alongside the
   deed.

5. Quitclaim Deed
   Transfers ownership of your home from "[Your Name]" to "The [Trust Name]
   Trust." Signed before witnesses + notary as your state requires. Recorded
   at county Register of Deeds.

6. Affidavit of Value (if required by your state)
   Filed alongside the deed. Claims any applicable exemption from the state's
   deed recording fee or transfer tax for transfers to a trust where the
   grantor is the beneficiary. Exemptions and specific forms vary by state.

7. Garn-St. Germain Notification Letter
   Sent to your mortgage lender informing them of the transfer. Cites
   12 U.S.C. § 1701j-3. Confirms you remain beneficiary and occupant. Not
   asking permission — informing.

8. Trustee Liability Waiver
   Private contract between you and the attorney eliminating personal liability
   for the attorney's actions as trustee. Makes the attorney comfortable
   serving in the role.
```

## What changes in your daily life (almost nothing)

Once everything is transferred, your day-to-day life is virtually identical. You still live in your house, drive your cars, pay your bills. The only differences:

**Mortgage payments** now come from the trust checking account instead of your personal account. Set up auto-pay and forget it.

**Property taxes** auto-pay from the trust account. The tax bill arrives at your PMB (or the attorney's office), not your home.

**Insurance** references the trust as the property owner. Your premiums don't change — the insurer still knows who you are internally for underwriting purposes.

**Vehicle registration renewals** are done online through your DMV's website using a Privacy.com card. The renewal notice goes to your PMB.

**When something needs the trustee's signature** — refinancing, selling a vehicle, an insurance claim — you contact your attorney, they sign, and you pay their per-signature fee. This happens maybe once or twice a year.

:::tip[The result]
Someone searches your name on the county property records website: **nothing**. Someone runs your license plate: **generic trust name at a PMB mail facility**. Someone pulls your credit report: **PMB address**. Someone searches you on a data broker site (after Optery does its work): **ghost address, no home connection**. You still own everything. You still control everything. You just don't exist on paper.
:::
